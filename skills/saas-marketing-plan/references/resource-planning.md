## Budget Allocation Frameworks

### The 70/20/10 Rule

A widely used framework for allocating marketing resources across risk levels:

- **70%** on proven channels — things you know work for you
- **20%** on scaling and optimizing — expanding what works, testing variations
- **10%** on experiments — new channels or approaches you haven't tried

This applies to both time and money. A founder with 10 hrs/week: 7 hours on their primary channel, 2 hours improving it, 1 hour trying something new.

### Budget Allocation by Stage

**Pre-revenue ($0 budget):**
All time goes to one organic channel. No experiments until you have signal. Don't split attention.

**Early revenue ($1,000–3,000/month marketing budget):**

| Allocation | Percentage | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| One paid channel | 100% | Validate and scale a single channel — do not split |

Do not add a second paid channel at this budget. Concentrate, validate, then scale.

**Growth stage ($3,000–10,000/month):**

| Allocation | Percentage | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Primary paid channel | 100% of paid budget | Continue scaling the proven channel toward $10K/month |

Stay focused on one paid channel. Add a second paid channel only when the first is past $10K/month or its performance is clearly hitting a ceiling (rising CPA, saturating audience, frequency too high). Organic channels (content, community, social, email) run in parallel on time investment, not budget.

**Scale stage ($10,000+/month):**

| Allocation | Percentage | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Primary paid channel | 60–70% | The proven workhorse |
| Second paid channel test | 15–20% | Minimum $1,000/month to generate meaningful data |
| Retargeting | 10–15% | Convert warm visitors from all channels |
| Sponsorships / experiments | 5–10% | Newsletter, podcast, community sponsorships |

Only reach this stage after your primary channel has stable, viable unit economics.

### When to Increase Budget

Only increase marketing budget when:
1. You have measurement in place (CAC and LTV by channel — route to `saas-data-analysis`)
2. At least one channel has viable unit economics (CAC < LTV/3)
3. Increasing spend would reach more audience, not just bid higher for the same people
4. You have product-market fit signal (40%+ "very disappointed" on the Sean Ellis question)

**When to add a second paid channel:** Only when your first paid channel is spending $10,000+/month with stable economics OR when performance is clearly hitting a ceiling (rising CPA, audience saturation, frequency fatigue). Adding a second paid channel below $10K total paid spend means neither channel gets enough data to optimize. Scale one channel well before diversifying.

Increasing budget without these conditions is spending money to learn things you could learn for free by talking to customers.

### The Testing Rule

Budget at least $1,000 to test a new paid channel. Anything less doesn't produce enough data to evaluate the channel fairly. If $1,000 on Google Ads produces zero conversions, the problem isn't budget — it's targeting, messaging, or channel fit. Diagnose before scaling.

**Exception:** LinkedIn Ads, where CPCs are high enough that $1,000 may only produce 75–150 clicks. Budget $1,500–$3,000 for a meaningful LinkedIn test.

### Diminishing Returns by Channel

Every channel hits a point where more spend produces diminishing results. Know where these ceilings are:

| Channel | What the ceiling looks like | What to do when you hit it |
|---|---|---|
| Google Ads (brand) | You're capturing 90%+ of brand search impressions | Don't increase — maintain spend |
| Google Ads (category) | CPC rising, impression share high, CPA increasing | Expand to adjacent keywords, not more budget on same keywords |
| Meta Ads | Frequency > 3, creative fatigued, CPA rising | New creative, not more budget. Expand audiences. |
| LinkedIn Ads | Audience saturation (frequency > 6), CPA climbing | New audiences, new geographies, or pause and revisit quarterly |
| Content/SEO | All core keywords covered, traffic growth flattening | Expand to adjacent topics, invest in link building, refresh old content |

## Resource Planning

### Time Estimates by Channel (per week, at Tier 2 quality)

| Channel | Hours/week | What fills the time |
|---|---|---|
| Content/SEO | 5–8 | Research, writing, editing, publishing, basic promotion |
| Google Ads | 2–3 | Campaign monitoring, keyword adjustments, ad copy testing |
| Meta Ads | 2–4 | Creative production, campaign monitoring, testing |
| LinkedIn Ads | 1–2 | Campaign monitoring, audience adjustments (less creative churn than Meta) |
| Social media (one platform) | 3–5 | Content creation, engagement, community interaction |
| Email marketing | 2–3 | Writing, segmentation, automation setup, analysis |
| Community | 3–5 | Participation, answering questions, relationship building |
| YouTube | 6–10 | Planning, recording, editing, publishing (highest time cost) |
| Partnerships | 2–3 | Outreach, coordination, content creation |
| Podcast guesting | 1–2 | Prep, recording, promotion (averaged across weeks) |
| PR | 1–2 | Relationship maintenance, pitch prep (spikes around launches) |

**Reality check:** A solo founder with 10 hrs/week for marketing can sustain 2 channels at Tier 2 quality. Three channels means at least one gets neglected. Be honest about capacity and choose accordingly.

### When to Hire vs. DIY

| Do it yourself when | Hire or outsource when |
|---|---|
| Finding product-market fit (stay close to the customer) | PMF found, need to scale a proven channel |
| Channel requires founder authenticity (building in public, thought leadership, community) | Channel is execution-heavy and repeatable (ads, content volume, video editing) |
| More time than money | More money than time |
| Still learning what messaging works | Messaging is proven, need more of it |
| Channel strategy is unclear | Strategy is clear, need execution capacity |

### What to Outsource First

Ranked by ease of outsourcing and quality maintenance:

1. **Graphic design** — ad creative, social graphics, landing page elements. Easy to brief, easy to evaluate, consistent output.
2. **Video editing** — give raw footage, get polished video. Clear deliverable.
3. **Content writing** — with your direction, outline, and review. Quality varies widely — invest time in finding a good writer. Expect to heavily edit the first 3–5 pieces until they learn your voice.
4. **Ad management** — PPC specialists can manage Google and Meta campaigns with your strategy direction. Ensure they report on metrics you care about (CAC, activation quality) not vanity metrics (impressions, clicks).
5. **Email template design and automation setup** — one-time setup that a specialist does faster than you.

### What to Keep In-House

- **Strategy and channel selection** — this is your judgment, not a freelancer's
- **Messaging and positioning** — requires deep product and customer knowledge
- **Community engagement** — your authentic voice and expertise matters
- **Customer conversations** — no one understands your product like you
- **Content review and direction** — even with a writer, you set the strategy and maintain quality

### Freelancer Budget Estimates

| Role | Monthly cost (part-time) | What they deliver |
|---|---|---|
| SaaS-savvy content writer | $1,000–3,000 | 4–8 articles per month, researched and SEO-optimized |
| PPC manager (Google + Meta) | $500–2,000 + ad spend | Campaign setup, monitoring, optimization, weekly reporting |
| Social media manager | $500–1,500 | Content creation, scheduling, basic engagement |
| Designer | $500–1,500 | Ad creative, social graphics, landing page design |
| Video editor | $500–2,000 | Edit raw footage into polished videos |
| Email marketing specialist | $500–1,500 | Sequence setup, template design, automation configuration |

These are ranges for competent freelancers on Upwork, Contra, or through referrals. Agencies charge 2–5x these rates. For most SaaS companies under $3M ARR, freelancers are the right choice — you get dedicated attention without agency overhead.

### Hiring Your First Marketing Person

When you're ready to hire (usually $1M+ ARR or after fundraising):

**What to hire first:** A generalist who can execute across 2–3 channels, not a specialist in one channel. At early stage, you need someone who can write a blog post on Monday, adjust Google Ads on Tuesday, and draft a newsletter on Thursday.

**Title:** "Head of Growth" or "Marketing Lead" — someone senior enough to own strategy but willing to do execution.

**What they need:** SaaS experience (mandatory), comfort with data and analytics, writing ability, and willingness to operate at Tier 1–2 sophistication before building a team.

**What they don't need:** Agency experience, big-company marketing background, or a team of specialists under them. Those come later.
