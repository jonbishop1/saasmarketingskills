# Copy Frameworks for SaaS Landing Pages

Fill-in-the-blank frameworks for founders who need the words. These aren't creative writing prompts — they're structural templates based on patterns that convert. Pick one, fill it in, and iterate from there.

## Headline Formulas

Each formula works. Pick the one that fits your product and audience. The formulas are ordered from simplest (best for Tier 1 / speed to launch) to most sophisticated (best for Tier 2+ with time to iterate).

### Formula 1: Direct description
**Pattern:** `[Action verb] [what] [for whom / in what context]`

Best when your product category is understood and you need pure clarity.

- API product: "Monitor your APIs in real time."
- SEO tool: "Find keywords your competitors rank for."
- Generic: "[Do thing] [that matters] [without the pain]."

### Formula 2: Outcome-first
**Pattern:** `[Desired outcome the customer wants]`

Best when the outcome is more compelling than the mechanism.

- API product: "Know when your API breaks before your customers do."
- SEO tool: "Rank for the keywords that actually drive revenue."
- Generic: "[The result they want], [without the thing they hate]."

### Formula 3: Pain → relief
**Pattern:** `Stop [pain]. Start [benefit].` or `[Pain statement]. [Product] fixes that.`

Best when the problem is universally felt and emotionally resonant.

- API product: "Stop finding out about outages from your customers."
- SEO tool: "Stop guessing which keywords to target."

### Formula 4: Specificity play
**Pattern:** `[Specific mechanism] for [specific audience]`

Best for developer tools and technical products where precision signals competence.

- API product: "Uptime monitoring with sub-second alerting for developer teams."
- SEO tool: "Keyword gap analysis that shows you exactly where to win."

### Formula 5: Social proof headline
**Pattern:** `[X customers/companies] use [product] to [outcome]`

Best when you have strong numbers and recognized users. Don't use this with weak numbers.

- API product: "200+ engineering teams monitor their APIs with PingForge."
- SEO tool: "5,000 SEO teams find their next ranking opportunity with KeyScope."

### Formula 6: Contrast / comparison
**Pattern:** `[Old way] → [New way with your product]`

Best when replacing an existing workflow or competitor.

- API product: "From 'the API is down' to 'the API was down for 4 seconds and we auto-recovered.'"
- SEO tool: "From spreadsheet keyword research to automated opportunity discovery."

### Choosing a formula

| Situation | Recommended formula |
|---|---|
| Pre-launch, need speed | Formula 1 (Direct) or Formula 2 (Outcome) |
| Developer audience | Formula 4 (Specificity) or Formula 1 (Direct) |
| Strong social proof | Formula 5 (Social proof headline) |
| Replacing a known competitor | Formula 6 (Contrast) |
| Emotional/pain-driven product | Formula 3 (Pain → relief) |
| Unsure | Formula 2 (Outcome) — it works for almost everything |

## Subheadline Patterns

The subheadline adds the context the headline omits. If the headline says what, the subheadline says who/how/why. If the headline says the outcome, the subheadline says the mechanism.

### Pattern A: Expand the headline
**Headline:** "Know when your API breaks before your customers do."
**Subheadline:** "Uptime monitoring with real-time alerts in Slack, PagerDuty, and webhooks. Set up in 30 seconds."

### Pattern B: Specify the audience
**Headline:** "Find the keywords your competitors rank for — and the ones they're missing."
**Subheadline:** "Built for SEO teams and content marketers who need data-driven keyword strategy, not vanity metrics."

### Pattern C: Address the objection
**Headline:** "Monitor your APIs in real time."
**Subheadline:** "No agents to install. No config files. Paste your endpoint and get alerts in under a minute."

### Pattern D: Social proof bump
**Headline:** "Rank for the keywords that actually drive revenue."
**Subheadline:** "Trusted by 2,000+ SEO professionals. 14-day free trial, no credit card required."

**Rule of thumb:** The headline + subheadline together should answer: What is this? Who is it for? Why should I care? If all three aren't covered between the two, something's missing.

## Feature Description Templates

Each feature card on the page needs: a name/headline, a 1–2 sentence description, and optionally a visual. The description must frame the feature as a benefit, not a spec.

### Template: Outcome framing
**Pattern:** `[Feature name]. [What it means for the user in outcome terms].`

- Bad: "Real-time monitoring. We check your endpoints every 30 seconds."
- Good: "Real-time monitoring. Know about outages within 30 seconds — before your customers notice."

- Bad: "Keyword gap analysis. Compare your domain against competitors."
- Good: "Keyword gap analysis. See exactly which keywords your competitors rank for that you're missing — and how hard they'd be to win."

### Template: Before/after framing
**Pattern:** `Without [product]: [pain]. With [product]: [relief].`

- "Without PingForge: You find out about outages from angry customer emails. With PingForge: You get an alert in Slack 30 seconds after the first failed health check."
- "Without KeyScope: Keyword research means hours in spreadsheets comparing domains manually. With KeyScope: Paste two URLs and get a prioritized list of keyword opportunities in 10 seconds."

### Template: Quantified improvement
**Pattern:** `[Feature name]. [Specific measurable improvement].`

Only use this if you have real numbers. Don't fabricate metrics.

- "Intelligent alerting. Cut alert noise by 80% with automatic deduplication and severity routing."
- "Opportunity scoring. Our ranking difficulty model is 2x more accurate than traditional keyword difficulty scores."

## Problem Statement Frameworks

The problem section validates the visitor's pain. It says "we understand your world." This builds trust before you pitch.

### Framework: Day-in-the-life
Describe the painful workflow the customer currently endures. Be specific enough that they recognize themselves.

**API product example:**
"Your monitoring setup is a patchwork of cron jobs, Pingdom checks, and hope. When something breaks at 2 AM, you find out from a customer tweet. You scramble to check dashboards, grep logs, and figure out which service actually failed. By the time you know the root cause, your status page still says 'all systems operational.'"

**SEO tool example:**
"You export keyword data from three different tools into a spreadsheet. You manually cross-reference competitor rankings. You guess at keyword difficulty based on domain authority and hope for the best. Two hours later, you have a list you're not confident in."

### Framework: Cost of status quo
Quantify the cost of not solving the problem.

- "Every minute of undetected downtime costs your team trust with customers you spent months acquiring."
- "Your content team publishes 10 articles a month. Without data-driven keyword targeting, 6 of those articles will never rank on page one."

### Framework: The gap
Name the gap between what exists and what should exist.

- "Monitoring tools built for infrastructure teams don't understand API-specific failure modes. You need monitoring that speaks HTTP, understands latency percentiles, and alerts on degradation — not just downtime."
- "Enterprise SEO tools cost $500+/month and bury the insights you need under dashboards you don't. Freelancers and small teams need the same data without the overhead."

## Social Proof Copy

### Testimonial coaching

If you're collecting testimonials from customers, give them structure. An unguided testimonial produces "Great product!" A guided testimonial produces conversion gold.

Ask customers to answer:
1. What problem were you trying to solve?
2. What did you try before this?
3. What specific result did you get?

**Template:** "[Specific result] — [Context that makes it credible]. [Product] [specific thing it did for them]."

Example: "We cut our mean time to detection from 12 minutes to under 30 seconds. PingForge caught a regional CDN failure that our internal monitoring completely missed." — Sarah Chen, VP Engineering, Acme Corp

Example: "We found 340 ranking opportunities our agency had missed entirely. KeyScope paid for itself in the first week." — Marcus Rivera, Head of SEO, GrowthStack

### Metric copy patterns

- "Trusted by [X] [role]s at [Y] companies"
- "Monitoring [X] endpoints across [Y] organizations"
- "Tracking [X] keywords for [Y] SEO teams"
- "[X]% average [improvement metric] for customers"

Only use metrics you can defend. Round numbers ("500+") feel more honest than exact numbers ("487") because exact numbers imply false precision for fast-growing metrics.

## CTA Copy Patterns

### Primary CTA formulas

| Pattern | Example | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Action + object | "Get your API key" | Developer tools |
| Action + timeframe | "Start monitoring in 30 seconds" | PLG with fast setup |
| Action + free signal | "Start free trial" | Standard PLG |
| Action + low commitment | "Book a 15-min demo" | Sales-led |
| Action + outcome | "Start ranking higher" | Content/SEO tools |

### Supporting text (below the button)

Always add a friction reducer below or near the CTA button:
- "No credit card required"
- "Free for up to [X] [units]"
- "Set up in under [X] minutes"
- "Cancel anytime"
- "14-day free trial"

Pick the one that addresses the biggest sign-up friction for your product. For most PLG SaaS, "no credit card required" is the highest-impact friction reducer.

## FAQ Writing Guide

Write FAQs as objection-handling in disguise. Each question should represent a real conversion barrier.

### Structure for each answer

1. **Direct answer first.** Don't hedge. "Yes, your data is encrypted at rest and in transit using AES-256."
2. **Supporting detail.** One sentence of context or proof. "We're SOC 2 Type II certified and undergo annual third-party audits."
3. **Link if relevant.** "See our security page for full details." Keep answers to 2–4 sentences.

### FAQ questions that actually matter (by product type)

**PLG / SaaS:** Do I need a credit card to start? What happens when my trial ends? Can I export my data? How long does setup take? What integrations do you support?

**Developer tools:** What's the rate limit? Is there an SLA? Do you support [language/framework]? Where are your servers located? Can I self-host?

**Enterprise:** How is my data stored and secured? What compliance certifications do you have? What does implementation look like? What's your uptime SLA? How does pricing scale?

**Content / SEO tools:** How fresh is the data? Where does the data come from? How accurate is the keyword difficulty score? Can I export reports? What makes this different from [competitor]?

## Copy Tiers by Founder Ability

### "I can't write copy" (Tier 1 copy)
Use Formula 1 or Formula 2 for the headline. Fill in the templates literally. Don't try to be clever. Clarity beats creativity. A clear, boring headline converts better than a clever, confusing one.

Your goal: the visitor can explain what you do to someone else after reading only the hero. That's it. Everything else is optimization.

### "I can write but it's not great" (Tier 2 copy)
Start with the templates, then improve. Read your copy aloud. If it sounds like a press release or a LinkedIn post, rewrite it. Good copy sounds like you explaining your product to a smart friend over coffee.

Test: Would an engineer on your team be slightly embarrassed by the copy? If yes, tone it down. If they'd be comfortable sharing it with a technical peer, you're in the right range.

### "I've written landing page copy before" (Tier 3 copy)
Skip the templates and focus on: message-market fit (does the headline match what your best customers actually say about you?), specificity (replace every vague claim with a specific one), and differentiation (does your copy sound like only your product, or could a competitor use the same headline?).

## Words and Phrases to Avoid

These phrases are so overused in SaaS that they've become invisible. Visitors' eyes skip right over them.

| Avoid | Why | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| "Revolutionary" / "Cutting-edge" | Meaningless hype | A specific capability |
| "Powerful" | Every product claims this | What the power enables |
| "Seamless integration" | No one believes this | "[Specific tool] integration in [time]" |
| "Best-in-class" | Unverifiable claim | A specific metric or comparison |
| "Leverage" / "Utilize" | Corporate speak | "Use" |
| "Solution" | Generic noun | Name the actual product type |
| "Empower" | Patronizing | What the user can now do |
| "Unlock" / "Unleash" | Cliché | The specific outcome |
| "Robust" | Means nothing | The specific technical quality |
| "At scale" | Often premature | Your actual scale numbers |

The test: if a competitor could swap in their product name and the copy still works, it's too generic. Good copy is specific to your product, your audience, and your differentiation.
