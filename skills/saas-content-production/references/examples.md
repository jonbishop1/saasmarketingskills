## Example 1: API Monitoring Product

### Scenario: PingForge

**The founder:** Solo technical founder. Senior backend engineer. Writes good documentation and clear technical posts (has a personal blog with 15 posts about distributed systems). No marketing content experience. Comfortable writing but slow — a blog post takes 4–5 hours.

**The product:** API uptime and latency monitoring. 5 months post-launch. ~80 paying customers. Has 6 blog posts — mostly deep technical content about API reliability.

**Content state:**
- 6 posts published over 5 months
- Top post: "Understanding API Latency Percentiles" — ranks #8 for the keyword, ~150 organic visits/month
- Other posts: minimal organic traction
- No comparison pages, no alternative pages, no use-case pages
- No content calendar or keyword map
- No repurposing — posts are published and forgotten

**Resources:** 3–4 hours/week for content. No budget for writers. No SEO tools (has Ahrefs Lite from SEO audit).

**What they asked:** "I need to write more blog posts but everything takes forever and I'm not sure what to write about. Can you help me write a comparison post for PingForge vs Better Uptime?"

---

### Situation Assessment

**Launch stage:** Post-launch, 5 months. → Building foundation. The 6 existing posts are mostly wrong-funnel (technical deep-dives instead of bottom-funnel conversion content). The request for a comparison page is exactly the right instinct.

**Who writes:** Founder writes everything. → Coach the process to reduce the 4–5 hour writing time. The time problem is likely drafting-while-editing (common for engineers who iterate on code constantly).

**Writing ability:** Good technical writer with a blog. → Strong foundation. Doesn't need writing instruction — needs process and structure. The shift from technical writing to content writing is small.

**Resources:** 3–4 hrs/week, no writer, no extra budget. → Tier 1 execution. One piece every 1–2 weeks. Must be high-impact.

**Content maturity:** 6 pieces, minimal organic traction on most. → Early foundation. The comparison page request is higher priority than any blog post. After that, alternative pages.

---

### Coaching: Speed Up the Writing Process

The founder takes 4–5 hours per post. Target: 2–2.5 hours.

**Diagnosis:** Almost certainly editing while drafting. Engineers refactor code as they write it — they do the same with prose. The fix:

1. **Brief first (20 min).** Using the quick brief template:
   ```
   Keyword: PingForge vs Better Uptime
   Who's reading: Developer or engineering lead evaluating monitoring tools
   What they need: Honest comparison to make a decision
   My angle: I've used Better Uptime. I know where it's strong and weak.
   Structure: [comparison page structure from content types guide]
   CTA: Start monitoring free
   Word count: ~1,500
   ```

2. **Outline (10 min).** H2 headings with bullet notes. Don't write sentences yet.

3. **Ugly first draft (45–60 min).** Set a timer. Write without editing. Don't fix typos. Don't rewrite sentences. If a section is hard, write "[TODO: explain pricing difference]" and move on. The goal is words on the page, not good words.

4. **Edit the next day (30–45 min).** Fresh eyes catch more. One pass for structure, one for substance, one for clarity.

**Total: ~2 hours.** The founder saves 2–3 hours per post, which means at 3–4 hours/week they can publish weekly instead of biweekly.

### Production: PingForge vs Better Uptime Comparison Page

#### Brief (created collaboratively)

```
Content type: Comparison page
Target keyword: "PingForge vs Better Uptime"
Secondary: "better uptime alternative", "api monitoring comparison"
Intent: Commercial investigation — actively choosing a tool
Audience: Developer or engineering lead at a team of 5-50

SERP analysis:
- No existing comparison page for this specific matchup
- General "api monitoring comparison" posts exist but are generic listicles
- Opportunity: specific, honest 1v1 comparison with real usage experience

Unique angle: Founder has personally used Better Uptime during evaluation.
Can speak to real setup experience, actual feature differences, and honest
tradeoffs. Not a marketing comparison — an engineer's comparison.

Structure:
- H1: PingForge vs Better Uptime: An Honest Comparison for Developer Teams
- Quick comparison table
- H2: What Better Uptime does well
- H2: What PingForge does differently
- H2: Setup experience
- H2: Monitoring capabilities
- H2: Alerting and integrations
- H2: Pricing
- H2: When to choose Better Uptime
- H2: When to choose PingForge
- CTA

CTA: Start monitoring free — 30-second setup
Internal links: Link to "Understanding API Latency Percentiles" post from
the monitoring capabilities section
Word count: ~1,500
```

#### Draft guidance

Key coaching points for this specific piece:

- **Lead with the comparison table.** The reader is decision-making, not browsing. Give them the summary first, details second.
- **"What Better Uptime does well" comes before "What PingForge does differently."** This signals honesty immediately. The reader's trust goes up, and everything you say about PingForge after is more credible.
- **Setup experience is a differentiator — show, don't tell.** If PingForge's 30-second setup is a real advantage, describe both experiences: "In Better Uptime, you create an account, add a monitor, configure check intervals, set up a status page, and configure alerting — about 5 minutes. In PingForge, you paste your endpoint URL and click 'Start Monitoring' — about 30 seconds."
- **Be specific in the pricing section.** Don't just say "PingForge is cheaper." Show the actual monthly cost for a comparable setup: "Monitoring 25 endpoints with 30-second checks: $X/month on Better Uptime, $Y/month on PingForge."
- **"When to choose Better Uptime" is genuine.** If the reader needs public status pages and incident management workflows, Better Uptime is the better fit. Say so. This makes "When to choose PingForge" — focused monitoring, developer-first UX, faster setup — much more compelling.

#### Post-publish

- Add internal link from the "Understanding API Latency Percentiles" post pointing to this comparison page
- Update the site's navigation or footer to include a "Compare" or "vs" link
- Repurposing (Tier 2 — 30 min): One Twitter thread (the "honest comparison" angle works well on Twitter) + one LinkedIn post

---

### Recommended Next 4 Pieces (in order)

| Piece | Type | Why this, why now |
|---|---|---|
| "Better Uptime Alternatives for Developer Teams" | Alternative page | Captures switching intent. Uses research from the comparison page. |
| "Datadog vs PingForge: When You Don't Need Full Observability" | Comparison page | Captures founders evaluating Datadog who are intimidated by complexity/cost. |
| "How to Monitor REST API Uptime: A Developer's Guide" | How-to guide | Mid-funnel, targets "how to monitor api" keywords. Positions PingForge naturally among solutions. |
| Refresh "Understanding API Latency Percentiles" | Content refresh | Already ranks #8 — with optimization could reach #3-5 and increase traffic 3-5×. |

This gives the founder 5 weeks of content (1 piece/week at ~2 hrs each) that covers bottom-funnel and one mid-funnel piece, plus one high-ROI refresh.

## Example 2: SEO Keyword Research Tool

### Scenario: KeyScope

**The founders:** Two-person team. Technical co-founder (full-stack) and growth co-founder (4 years marketing, including content). Growth co-founder writes most content. They also have a freelance writer producing 2 posts/month at $0.20/word.

**The product:** Keyword research tool focused on gap analysis and opportunity scoring. 10 months post-launch. 340 paying customers. 23 blog posts.

**Content state:**
- 23 published posts (mix of educational and tactical)
- Top 3 posts drive 70% of organic traffic
- Freelancer content is "okay" — factually correct but lacks the depth and originality of founder-written posts
- Comparison pages exist for Ahrefs and Semrush but not for mid-market competitors (Mangools, SE Ranking, Ubersuggest)
- Growth co-founder writes 1 post/week, freelancer adds 2/month
- Light repurposing: occasional LinkedIn post, no Twitter threads, no newsletter

**Resources:** Growth co-founder dedicates ~6 hours/week to content. Freelancer at $800/month. Has Ahrefs.

**What they asked:** "Our freelancer content isn't performing as well as what I write. I think we need better briefs. Also, we want to start repurposing more seriously but don't know where to start."

---

### Situation Assessment

**Launch stage:** Growth phase, 10 months, 23 posts, growing organic traffic. → Full production system. Both content creation and operations improvements are warranted.

**Who writes:** Growth co-founder (primary) + freelancer (supplementary). → Two production problems: (1) freelancer quality gap, (2) repurposing system missing. Both are solvable.

**Writing ability:** Growth co-founder is experienced — not the bottleneck. → Don't coach writing fundamentals. Focus on systems: brief quality, review process, repurposing workflow.

**Resources:** ~6 hrs/week co-founder + $800/mo freelancer. → Tier 2 capacity with Tier 3 ambitions. The freelancer budget is correctly sized for 2 posts/month at $0.20/word (~2,000 words each). The question is whether those posts earn their cost.

**Content maturity:** 23 pieces, clear performance distribution. → Ready for systematic improvement. The top 3 posts prove the content model works. The underperforming freelancer posts suggest a brief/quality problem, not a strategy problem.

---

### Solving the Freelancer Quality Gap

**Diagnosis:** "Factually correct but lacks depth and originality" is the most common freelancer complaint in SaaS. It's almost always a brief problem, not a writer problem.

**The current brief is probably:** A topic, a target keyword, a word count, and maybe a few bullet points. The freelancer researches by reading the top 5 Google results and produces a synthesis. The output is correct but unremarkable — it's a summary of existing content, not a new contribution.

**The fix:** Enhanced briefs that include the founder's expertise.

Before:
```
Topic: How to Find Low-Competition Keywords
Keyword: "low competition keywords"
Word count: 1,800
Notes: Cover what makes keywords low competition, how to find them, tools to use
```

After:
```
Topic: How to Find Low-Competition Keywords
Keyword: "low competition keywords"
Word count: 1,800
Intent: Informational — reader wants a practical method
Audience: SEO beginner to intermediate, likely at a small company

SERP analysis:
- Top results are generic listicles with obvious advice ("use long-tail keywords")
- Missing: actual methodology with examples, realistic difficulty assessment
- Opportunity: show a real workflow with real keyword examples and data

Key points to cover:
1. Why "low competition" is relative to YOUR domain (a DR 25 site vs DR 60 site
   have different "low competition" thresholds) — this is our core thesis
2. The free method: Search Console → find keywords you rank 20-50 for → these are
   proven low-competition for your domain
3. The tool method: keyword gap analysis showing competitor keywords with low KD
   where you have topical relevance
4. Real examples: [provide 3-4 actual keyword examples with real difficulty scores
   and volume from KeyScope's data]

Unique angle / founder expertise:
- Include this insight: "Traditional KD scores don't work because they're absolute,
  not relative. A keyword with KD 35 is 'low competition' for a DR 60 site but
  'medium competition' for a DR 20 site. KeyScope's opportunity score factors in
  your domain's authority — that's why it's more accurate."
- Include this data point: "In our analysis of 50,000 keywords, traditional KD
  scores predicted ranking difficulty correctly only 41% of the time when not
  adjusted for the searcher's domain authority."

CTA: Soft — mention KeyScope's opportunity scoring as one tool in the "tool method"
section. Don't force it.

Internal links:
- Link to "Keyword Gap Analysis Explained" post
- Link to "KeyScope vs Ahrefs" comparison page from the tools section
- After publishing, add a link from the "Complete Guide to Keyword Research" to
  this post
```

The enhanced brief takes 30–45 minutes to create instead of 5 minutes. But the output transforms: the freelancer now has specific data points, a unique angle, and real examples that can't be found by googling. The resulting post reads like it was written by someone with domain expertise — because the expertise was injected at the brief stage.

**Review process upgrade:**

Current: Freelancer submits → growth co-founder reads → publishes or asks for revisions.

Improved:
1. Freelancer submits draft
2. Review against the quality rubric (from SKILL.md):
   - Intent match? (Does it answer what the searcher needs?)
   - Substance? (Does it contain insights beyond the first 5 Google results?)
   - Expertise signal? (Does it include the data/insights from the brief?)
   - Specificity? (Concrete numbers, examples, product names?)
3. One round of specific feedback referencing the rubric
4. Freelancer revises
5. Growth co-founder does a final pass for voice consistency
6. Publish

Expected improvement: Freelancer posts should reach 70–80% of founder-written quality within 2–3 articles using enhanced briefs. The remaining gap is voice and nuance — acceptable for supplementary content.

---

### Setting Up the Repurposing System

**Current state:** Occasional LinkedIn post, nothing systematic.

**Recommended: Tier 2 repurposing (30 min per post)**

For every post published (founder-written or freelancer):

1. **Twitter/X thread (15 min):** Extract the core thesis + 3–4 supporting insights. The growth co-founder does this — they know the material and can add personal context.

2. **LinkedIn post (10 min):** One insight from the post, framed as a lesson or observation. Professional angle.

3. **Newsletter mention (5 min):** 2–3 sentence summary in the next newsletter issue with a link.

**Weekly repurposing block:** Schedule 1 hour every Friday for repurposing that week's content. Batch it — don't try to repurpose in the moment of publishing.

**Social snippets (add when comfortable):** After 2–3 weeks of thread + LinkedIn, start extracting 3–4 standalone snippets per post and scheduling them over 2 weeks. This adds ~15 min per post but extends the content's social reach significantly.

### Recommended Next 4 Pieces (in order)

| Piece | Writer | Type | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| "KeyScope vs Mangools" | Growth co-founder | Comparison | Mid-market competitor, no existing comparison page. Bottom-funnel. |
| "How to Find Low-Competition Keywords" | Freelancer (enhanced brief) | How-to guide | First test of the improved brief process. High-volume keyword. |
| "Mangools Alternatives for Small SEO Teams" | Freelancer (enhanced brief) | Alternative page | Bottom-funnel, pairs with the comparison page. |
| Refresh: "Best Keyword Research Tools" post | Growth co-founder | Content refresh | Currently ranks #11 — close to page 1. Highest ROI single action. |

This gives the team 3–4 weeks of content with a mix of founder-written bottom-funnel and freelancer mid-funnel, plus the highest-ROI refresh opportunity. The freelancer pieces test the enhanced brief process with real output.

### Expected Results After 3 Months

With the improved production system:
- Content pace: 5–6 pieces/month (1/week from co-founder + 2/month from freelancer)
- Freelancer quality: noticeably improved, closer to founder quality
- Repurposing: every post gets a thread + LinkedIn post + newsletter mention
- New comparison/alternative pages: 4–6 covering all mid-market competitors
- Content refreshes: 2–3 of the top-performing posts updated
- Organic traffic impact: 30–50% increase from bottom-funnel pages + refreshes
- Repurposing impact: 2–3× social engagement from systematic distribution
