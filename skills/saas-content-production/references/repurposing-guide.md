## Repurposing Principles

### Repurposing is not reposting

Copying a blog post's first paragraph into a LinkedIn post is not repurposing — it's lazy reposting, and it performs accordingly. Each channel has native conventions. Repurposing means extracting the core ideas and reformatting them for how people consume content on each channel.

### The extraction mindset

Every blog post contains multiple smaller ideas:
- A core thesis or argument
- 3–5 supporting points or insights
- Specific data points or statistics
- Practical tips or steps
- A counterintuitive take or opinion

Each of these can become a standalone social post, thread segment, or email section. You're not shrinking the blog post — you're pulling out atoms that work independently.

### What's worth repurposing

Not every blog post deserves full repurposing. Prioritize:
- **Content with a strong opinion or thesis** → makes great threads and LinkedIn posts
- **Content with data or specific numbers** → makes great social snippets
- **Content with practical steps** → makes great short-form how-to content
- **Content that performed well organically** → already validated, worth amplifying

Skip repurposing for:
- Commodity content that doesn't contain original insights
- Highly technical content that doesn't translate to social formats
- Time-sensitive content that will be stale in a week

## Twitter/X Threads

### Format conventions
- 4–10 tweets in a thread (sweet spot: 5–7)
- First tweet is the hook — must stand alone and earn the click to "Show more"
- Each tweet is one idea, one point
- Last tweet is the CTA (link to blog post, product, or ask for engagement)
- Use line breaks within tweets for readability
- Numbers, lists, and concrete examples perform best

### Template

```
Tweet 1 (hook):
[Bold claim, surprising stat, or relatable problem]

Here's what [I/we] learned [from doing X / analyzing Y / building Z]:

Tweet 2-6 (one insight per tweet):
[Insight number]. [Concise point]

[1-2 sentences of supporting evidence or example]

Tweet 7 (CTA):
[Summary of the key takeaway]

Full breakdown with [details/data/examples]: [link]
```

### What makes a good hook tweet
- A specific number: "We analyzed 1M API calls. 63% of outages happen between 2-6 AM."
- A counterintuitive claim: "Most keyword difficulty scores are wrong. Here's why."
- A relatable frustration: "You wake up to a Slack alert. Your API has been down for 47 minutes. Nobody noticed until a customer complained."
- A strong opinion: "If your SaaS blog has 30 posts and zero comparison pages, you're leaving money on the table."

## LinkedIn Posts

### Format conventions
- 150–300 words (shorter than a blog post, longer than a tweet)
- First 2 lines are the hook (this is what shows before "...see more")
- One core idea, not a thread of many
- Personal perspective works well — "I" not "we" (unless the brand account has an established voice)
- End with a question or engagement prompt
- No hashtag spam — 0–3 relevant hashtags maximum

### Template

```
[Hook line — strong claim, question, or observation]

[1-2 sentences expanding on the hook]

[Core insight — the substance of the post. 3-5 sentences with a specific example, number, or story.]

[Practical takeaway — what the reader should do with this information]

[Engagement prompt or CTA]
```

### LinkedIn vs. Twitter differences

| Dimension | Twitter/X | LinkedIn |
|---|---|---|
| Audience mindset | Browsing, entertainment, speed | Professional development, industry news |
| Tone | Casual, punchy, opinionated | Professional but personal, insight-driven |
| Best content | Hot takes, data drops, tactical tips | Lessons learned, career/company stories, frameworks |
| CTA style | Link to content | Ask a question, invite discussion |
| Length | Short (thread format for long content) | Single post, 150–300 words |

## Email Newsletter Segments

### Format conventions
- A repurposed blog post is one section of a newsletter, not the entire email
- 100–200 words summarizing the key insight
- Link to the full post for readers who want more
- Frame it as a personal recommendation, not a promotion

### Template

```
📌 [Section heading — not the blog post title, reframed for email]

[1-2 sentence personal intro: why this matters, why you wrote it]

[The core insight in 2-3 sentences — the reason someone should click through]

[Read the full [post/guide/breakdown] → [link]]
```

### Example framing
Don't: "We published a new blog post about API monitoring best practices. Read it here."
Do: "Last week we saw a customer catch a 47-minute outage because they'd set up 30-second check intervals instead of the default 5-minute ones. That finding turned into a deeper look at what actually works in API monitoring — and what most guides get wrong."

The second version gives the reader a reason to care before asking them to click.

## Video Scripts

### When to repurpose into video
- The content explains a process visually (screen recordings, diagrams)
- The founder is comfortable on camera or doing screen-share walkthroughs
- The content performs well in written form (validate before investing in video)
- The topic benefits from demonstration over explanation

### Format: Talking-head + screen share (most common for SaaS)

```
[0:00-0:15] Hook
"[State the problem or result upfront — what will the viewer learn?]"

[0:15-0:45] Context
"[Brief background — why this matters, who this is for]"

[0:45-3:00] Core content (screen share)
"[Walk through the process, showing the product/tool/method]"
- Step 1: [show and explain]
- Step 2: [show and explain]
- Step 3: [show and explain]

[3:00-3:30] Key takeaway + CTA
"[Summarize the main insight. Point to the full blog post or product.]"
```

### Video production tiers

| Tier | What's feasible | Tools needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1: Screen recording** | Record your screen walking through the content. No editing. Loom or similar. | Loom (free tier) |
| **Tier 2: Light editing** | Screen recording + cut dead air + add text overlays. 5–8 min videos. | Loom + Descript or CapCut |
| **Tier 3: Produced** | Talking head + screen share, edited with cuts, graphics, chapters. | Camera + editing software + time |

For most SaaS founders, Tier 1 or 2 is sufficient. Don't let production quality prevent you from publishing. A clear Loom walkthrough with useful content outperforms a polished video with nothing to say.

## Social Snippets

### What they are
Individual stats, tips, quotes, or insights pulled from a blog post and posted as standalone social content. These extend the content's reach over days/weeks instead of a single post-day spike.

### Format
- One idea per snippet
- Can be text-only or text + simple image/graphic
- Scheduled over 1–2 weeks after the blog post publishes
- Works on any platform (Twitter, LinkedIn, Threads, Mastodon)

### Extraction method
Read through the blog post and highlight:
- Any sentence with a specific number ("63% of API outages happen overnight")
- Any concrete tip ("Set check intervals to 30 seconds for production APIs")
- Any strong opinion ("Most keyword difficulty metrics are fundamentally broken")
- Any before/after comparison ("5-minute checks miss 40% of incidents")

Each highlight becomes one social snippet. A good blog post yields 3–8 snippets.

### Template

```
[The insight, stated directly — no preamble]

[1-2 sentences of context if needed]

[Optional: "From our latest [guide/analysis/research] — link in bio/comments"]
```

## Community Posts

### Where to post
- Hacker News (Show HN for launches, regular posts for insights)
- Reddit (relevant subreddits — r/SaaS, r/startups, industry-specific subs)
- Indie Hackers
- Dev.to / Hashnode (for developer audience)
- Industry-specific communities (Slack groups, Discord servers, forums)

### Community posting principles
- **Don't promote — contribute.** Communities detect and punish self-promotion. Share the insight, not the product.
- **Adapt to the community's norms.** Hacker News values technical depth. Reddit values authenticity. Indie Hackers values transparency about business metrics.
- **Engage with comments.** Posting and disappearing is a signal that you only care about the traffic. Stay and discuss.
- **Link to content only when it genuinely adds value.** "I wrote a guide on this, might help: [link]" in response to a relevant discussion is fine. "Check out our blog: [link]" as a top-level post is spam.

## Repurposing Workflow by Tier

### Tier 1: Zero repurposing (just publish the blog post)
Perfectly valid. The blog post still compounds via SEO. Better to publish consistently than burn out trying to repurpose everything.

### Tier 2: Quick repurposing (30 minutes per post)
1. Write one Twitter/X thread (15 min)
2. Write one LinkedIn post (10 min)
3. Add a summary section to your next newsletter (5 min)

### Tier 3: Full repurposing (1–2 hours per post)
1. Twitter/X thread (15 min)
2. LinkedIn post (10 min)
3. Newsletter segment (5 min)
4. Extract 3–5 social snippets, schedule over 2 weeks (20 min)
5. Post to 1–2 relevant communities if appropriate (15 min)

### Tier 4: Maximum repurposing (2+ hours or has help)
Everything in Tier 3, plus:
1. Record a video walkthrough or talking-head version (30–45 min)
2. Cross-post adapted version to Dev.to or Medium (15 min)
3. Create a slide deck version for LinkedIn carousel or SlideShare (20 min)

## Worked Example: PingForge

### Source: Blog post — "How to Monitor REST API Uptime: A Developer's Guide"

Key ideas to extract:
1. 30-second check intervals catch 40% more incidents than 5-minute intervals
2. Most monitoring tools require complex configuration — PingForge takes 30 seconds
3. The three monitoring approaches: DIY scripts, open-source tools, SaaS platforms
4. Silent API degradation (latency creeping up without triggering alert thresholds) is the most dangerous failure mode
5. Monitoring from multiple regions catches localized outages that single-region checks miss

### Twitter thread

```
Tweet 1:
We analyzed our monitoring data across 500+ API endpoints.

The difference between 30-second and 5-minute check intervals?

30-second checks caught 40% more incidents.

Here's what else we learned about API monitoring that most guides get wrong:

Tweet 2:
1/ Silent degradation is more dangerous than outages.

Your API returns 200 OK but latency has crept from 120ms to 800ms.

No alert fires. Users start churning.

Track P95 and P99 latency, not just up/down status.

Tweet 3:
2/ DIY monitoring scripts feel free but cost more.

A cron job + curl is simple until:
- The monitoring server goes down
- You need multi-region checks
- You need historical latency data
- You're on call and need reliable alerting

Sometimes "build vs buy" has a clear answer.

Tweet 4:
3/ Check from multiple regions or don't bother.

A single-region health check won't catch:
- CDN edge failures
- DNS propagation issues
- Regional cloud provider outages

If your users are global, your monitoring should be too.

Tweet 5:
4/ The best monitoring setup takes < 5 minutes.

Add your endpoint URL. Set check interval to 30 seconds.
Route alerts to Slack. Done.

Full guide with all three approaches (DIY, open-source, SaaS): [link]
```

### LinkedIn post

```
Most API monitoring setups have a dangerous blind spot:

They check if the API is "up" but ignore whether it's actually fast enough.

We looked at our monitoring data and found that silent latency degradation — where response times creep from 120ms to 800ms without triggering any alert — is behind more user complaints than actual outages.

The fix is simple: track P95 and P99 latency, not just HTTP status codes. Set alerts for latency thresholds, not just downtime.

One number that surprised us: switching from 5-minute to 30-second check intervals caught 40% more incidents. Most were brief latency spikes that resolved within 3 minutes — invisible to the standard 5-minute check.

What's your monitoring check interval set to? Curious what others have found works.
```

### Social snippets (schedule over 2 weeks)

```
Snippet 1: "30-second API monitoring checks catch 40% more incidents than 5-minute checks. Most incidents resolve within 3 minutes — invisible to standard intervals."

Snippet 2: "Silent API degradation > outages. Your API returns 200 OK but latency has crept from 120ms to 800ms. No alert fires. Track P95/P99, not just status codes."

Snippet 3: "If your API serves users in multiple regions, monitoring from a single location won't catch: CDN edge failures, DNS propagation issues, or regional cloud outages."

Snippet 4: "The monitoring setup that actually works: endpoint URL + 30-second intervals + Slack alerts. Under 5 minutes. No agents, no config files."
```

## Worked Example: KeyScope

### Source: Blog post — "How to Find Keywords Your Competitors Rank For"

Key ideas to extract:
1. 80% of keyword opportunities are hiding in your competitors' rankings, not in keyword suggestion tools
2. Manual competitor analysis (free) vs. tool-based gap analysis — both work, different depth
3. Most founders target keywords that are too competitive — long-tail competitor gaps are where the opportunity is
4. The "difficulty score lie" — traditional KD scores don't account for your domain's specific authority
5. A 15-minute keyword gap analysis can reveal 3 months of content ideas

### Twitter thread

```
Tweet 1:
Stop brainstorming blog topics.

Your competitors already did the keyword research for you.

80% of the best keyword opportunities are hiding in what your competitors rank for and you don't.

Here's how to find them (free and paid methods):

Tweet 2:
1/ Free method: manual gap analysis.

Search your 3 main competitors' blogs. Note every topic they've written about.

Now check: which of those topics do you NOT have content for?

That's your content gap list. Takes 30 minutes for a basic version.

Tweet 3:
2/ Paid method: keyword gap tools.

Plug your domain + 2-3 competitor domains into Ahrefs or a keyword gap tool.

Filter for: keywords where they rank top 20 and you don't.

Sort by volume × relevance. You now have 3-6 months of validated content ideas.

Tweet 4:
3/ The mistake most founders make:

They see a competitor ranking for "project management software" (100k/mo searches) and think that's the opportunity.

It's not. You can't compete for that.

The opportunity is "project management for engineering teams" (1k/mo, low competition).

Tweet 5:
4/ Keyword difficulty scores lie to you.

A keyword with "difficulty: 45" means nothing without context.

What matters: can YOUR domain rank for it?

A DR 25 site targeting a KD 45 keyword is a different bet than a DR 60 site.

Always factor in your own authority.

Tweet 6:
Full guide with step-by-step for both methods (free + paid): [link]
```

### LinkedIn post

```
I talk to a lot of SaaS founders who say "I don't know what to blog about."

Here's a framework that takes 15 minutes and gives you 3 months of content ideas:

Look at what your competitors rank for in Google, but you don't.

That's it. That's the strategy.

Your competitors have already validated which topics drive organic traffic in your space. You don't need to brainstorm or guess — you need to find the gaps and fill them.

The key insight most founders miss: don't target the same high-volume keywords your competitors dominate. Target the long-tail variants where they rank but haven't invested heavily.

"API monitoring" is a 50k/month keyword dominated by Datadog. You won't rank.

"API monitoring for microservices" is a 2k/month keyword with thinner competition. You can rank for that in 3-6 months with one solid piece of content.

What's the most surprising keyword gap you've found in your competitor analysis?
```
